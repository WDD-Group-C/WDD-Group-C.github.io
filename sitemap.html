Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) and their role in biodiversity conservation

Marine Protected Areas are the sections of the ocean that are limited from human activity by the government, making it helpful to protect and conserve the ocean ecosystems around the globe as they act as a shield to marine species from events such as overfishing, pollution, and habitat destruction (Laffoley, D., et al, 2019). The types of MPAs include marine reserves, marine sanctuaries, wildlife refuges and national parks.


Different types of MPAs range from zones that prohibit all extractive activities to zones allowing some human activities. some types are namely, marine parks, marine sanctuaries, etc. No-take zones (prohibit all activities) are exceptionally effective in protecting marine habits and biodiversity as it gives marine life to recover and flourish without being interfered by human actitvities (Coral Reef Alliance, 1994).

Phoenix Islands Protected Area is the largest Marine Protecting Area in the world, located in Kiribati. it covers 410,500 square kilometers (158,453 square miles), apprimately the size of the U.S. state of California (National Geographic, 1888).


Looking forward, the establishment of new no-take zones, such as the one planned in Roatan, Honduras, highlights the ongoing commitment to preserving critical reef ecosystems through collaborative conservation efforts. By partnering with local communities, dive shops, and government bodies, these initiatives minimizes overfishing and poaching. This collective action aims to ensure the thriving of marine life for future generations, fostering a sense of shared responsibility and stewardship for our oceans (National Geographic, 1888).
